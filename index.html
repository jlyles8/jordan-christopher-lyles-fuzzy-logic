Project Overview

The project aims to develop a personalized medication dosage adjustment and medication allocation system leveraging fuzzy logic principles. Medication dosage adjustment is a critical aspect of healthcare because the selected dose and medication can influence therapeutic outcomes, adverse effects, accessibility, and patient adherence. Traditional approaches often rely on fixed guidelines and broad population-level rules. Those guidelines are necessary, but they may not fully reflect individual patient characteristics such as age, body size, medical history, symptom severity, affected body region, medication cost, and drug-specific contraindications.

This project reframes medication selection as a multi-factor decision-support problem. Instead of treating medication choice as a single yes-or-no decision, the system assigns degrees of suitability across clinical and practical dimensions. Fuzzy logic is useful here because real patient data is often imprecise: pain may be moderate-to-severe, cost may be acceptable but not ideal, and adverse-effect profiles may be tolerable in one patient but inappropriate in another.

Why Fuzzy Logic?

Fuzzy logic is a form of mathematical reasoning that allows variables to belong to categories by degree rather than by strict binary membership. In a conventional system, a medication might be classified as either appropriate or inappropriate. In a fuzzy system, the same medication can be partially desirable depending on its pain effectiveness, adverse effect risk, cost score, and relevance to the patient's pain location.

Clinical uncertainty

Medication selection rarely depends on a single exact threshold. A patient can be between risk categories, and patient-reported symptoms often vary. Fuzzy logic allows the model to translate continuous scores into linguistic categories such as poor, average, good, low, medium, and high.

Human-like reasoning

The system mirrors clinical reasoning: a medication with high effectiveness and low cost may be desirable, but that recommendation can be weakened if adverse effects are severe or if the medication is not relevant to the affected body part.

Membership idea: a score does not simply pass or fail. It can belong partly to multiple sets, then rules aggregate those memberships into a final desirability score.

How Determinations Were Made

1. Build patient profiles.

The patient generator creates synthetic patient profiles with age, BMI, gender, race, pain duration, pain severity, sleep habits, workout frequency, water intake, smoking frequency, eating frequency, and caffeine intake. These values simulate the type of patient-specific data that could later be pulled from intake forms or electronic health records.

2. Score medication attributes.

Each medication in the project code is assigned numeric scores for ease of use, cost, accessibility, pain treatment effectiveness, adverse effects, duration, and affected body parts. These scores act as model inputs and approximate the practical tradeoffs that influence medication desirability.

3. Check body-part relevance.

Before fuzzy scoring, the code compares the patient's pain location against the medication's listed affected body parts. If the medication does not match the patient's pain location, the model labels it unsuitable for that case.

4. Apply exclusion criteria.

The medical exclusion criteria dataset adds a safety layer. It records age exclusions, disease-specific flags, bleeding risks, skin-related restrictions, opioid-risk concerns, and duration limits. These criteria explain why a medication may be filtered or flagged before final recommendation.

5. Compute fuzzy desirability.

The fuzzy inference system evaluates pain treatment effectiveness, adverse effects, and cost. The output is a medication desirability score from 0 to 100, normalized in the printed sample output to a 0 to 1 scale.

Medical Exclusion Criteria

The exclusion criteria dataset is included to make the model more clinically responsible. A high fuzzy desirability score should not automatically override safety concerns. For example, a medication can appear effective and affordable but still be inappropriate for a patient with a contraindication such as age restriction, bleeding risk, kidney disease, liver disease, seizure history, substance abuse risk, respiratory conditions, or skin sensitivity.
